# Composite-protected paths through visible lattice points: reductions, obstructions, and computations on Erdős problem #1212

(Draft v1, 2026-06-10. Autonomous research artifact: orchestration/verification/computation by Claude
(Anthropic), mathematical collaboration by GPT-5.5 Pro (OpenAI); human involvement non-significant.
All "Theorem/Lemma" items below carry tier tags: T2 = Lean-verified core, T1 = complete human proof
independently re-derived, T0 = machine-verified computation.)

## 1. The problem

Erdős [Er80, p.114] (= erdosproblems.com #1212, OPEN, no prior partial results): let G be the graph on
visible lattice points {(x,y) ∈ Z²_{>0} : gcd(x,y) = 1}, with edges between points differing by 1 in
exactly one coordinate. Does G contain an infinite path all of whose vertices satisfy

  (i) min(x,y) > 1, and (ii) at least one of x, y composite?

Stewart's prime-pair construction solves the weaker version (condition (i) only); condition (ii) bans its
both-prime anchors. No literature exists on the strengthened question.

## 2. Positive direction: the Composite-Anchor reduction

**Lemma 2.1 (Composite-Anchor Lemma; T1, cores T2).** Suppose a₁ < a₂ < ⋯ are composite integers with
  (V_n) gcd(a_n, s) = 1 for all s ∈ [a_{n+1}, a_{n+2}];
  (H_n) gcd(s, a_{n+2}) = 1 for all s ∈ [a_n, a_{n+1}].
Then the L-paths (a_n, a_{n+1}) → (a_n, a_{n+2}) → (a_{n+1}, a_{n+2}) concatenate into an infinite path
witnessing YES for #1212. (Per-vertex validity: Lean theorems `vertical_leg_valid`,
`horizontal_leg_valid` in erdos1212_cores.lean — compiled, 0 sorry, axioms ⊆ {propext, Classical.choice,
Quot.sound}.)

**Lemma 2.2 (clearance formula; T1).** Since a_n ≡ 0 mod each of its prime factors, condition (V_n) is
equivalent to: a_{n+2} − a_n < P⁻(a_n) (smallest prime factor), provided a_{n+1} − a_n < P⁻(a_n).
Hence the chain requires rough composite anchors whose least prime factor exceeds the next two gaps,
plus the sparse condition (H_n) (no prime factor of a_{n+2} has a multiple in [a_n, a_{n+1}]).

**Computation 2.3 (T0).** A greedy chain with 2000-rough composite anchors starting at 10⁷ runs for
797,568 anchors (reaching 6.7×10⁷) with ZERO backtracking; every vertex of every L-path triple was
machine-verified (797,566 triples, 0 violations). An alternative run reached 1.07×10⁸. Code + chain:
problems/1212/experiments/anchor_tree/.

## 3. Negative direction: no periodic certificate

A natural route to discharge Lemma 2.1's infinitely many conditions is a periodic certificate
(suggested by GPT-5.5 Pro): a finite path Γ closing up to translation by (gh, gv), with Δ_i = h·y_i −
v·x_i ≠ 0, rad(Δ_i) | g, no prime of g dividing both coordinates, and a witness prime of g dividing
exactly one coordinate at every vertex. Translates of Γ then concatenate to a YES-path (re-derived,
T1). We prove this route is void:

**Theorem 3.1 (No-Periodic-Certificate; T1, core T2).** For every g, h, v (gcd(h,v)=1) there is NO
finite path satisfying the certificate conditions.

*Proof sketch.* (Case g odd) rad(Δ)|g forces every Δ_i odd; steps change Δ by v or h, one of which is
odd, so one step-direction is banned entirely, contradicting the required displacement in both
coordinates. (Case 2|g) Let m = rad(g). Any vertex with x ≡ 0 (mod m) is isolated among protected
vertices: its horizontal neighbours have both coordinates coprime to m and both odd — witness-free
(Lean: `right_neighbor_witness_free`, `left_neighbor_witness_free`); its vertical neighbours are
both-even (Lean: `vertical_neighbor_both_even`). A certificate's net x-advance is gh ≥ m, and a
±1-step walk attains every intermediate x-value (Lean: `walk_intermediate_value`), in particular some
x ≡ 0 (mod m) — at a vertex that is invalid or isolated. ∎

**Corroboration (T0).** Exhaustive SCC searches of certificate state graphs — P ⊆ {2,3,5,7,11},
all coprime (h,v) ≤ 10, |Δ| ≤ 20000, with backtracking — find every cycle has zero displacement.
Completeness: every certificate-cycle edge needs a 3-term-AP window of P-smooth numbers (step ≤ 10);
enumeration of all 120,895 11-smooth numbers ≤ 10¹⁵ shows all such windows have top ≤ 1000 (extremals
d·{98,99,100}); hence the searches were cycle-complete: an independent exhaustion proof of Theorem 3.1
for those parameter ranges.

## 4. The precise obstruction, and a conditional resolution

**Proposition 4.1 (supply target; T1 analysis).** The chain of Lemma 2.2 closes unconditionally if:
(R1) there exist θ < δ < 1/2 with: every interval [x, x + x^θ] (x large) contains a composite b with
P⁻(b) > x^δ. Two further sufficient variants: (R2) a non-clumping refinement of almost-all
short-interval theorems; (R3) an explicit structured anchor family.

**Status of R1–R3 (GPT-5.5 Pro literature survey + our checks).** R1 is not available in the current
literature: every-interval P₂/E₂ theorems either lack least-prime-factor control or require interval
exponents > 1/2. The closest published result is Matomäki [JLMS 2022, arXiv:2012.11565, Thm 1.1]
(almost all x: (x − h log X, x] contains integers with Ω ≤ 2 and all prime factors > X^{1/8};
exceptional measure O(X/h)) and Matomäki–Teräväinen [TAMS 2023, arXiv:2207.05038] (E₂ in almost all
(x, x + log^{2.1} x]); both are almost-all statements whose exceptional sets are not known to avoid
clumping into a full window (R2 open); no known explicit family survives the block transitions (R3).
No choice of h closes the gap (spacing-vs-budget mismatch at every h).

**Corollary 4.2 (conditional YES; T1).** Under a Maier–Pomerance-type max-gap hypothesis for rough
composites — gaps between consecutive X^δ-rough composites below X are O(X^θ) for some θ < δ — Erdős
#1212 has answer YES, via Lemmas 2.1–2.2 (the H-condition is dodgeable by a counting argument since
anchor factors exceed X^δ while intervals have length O(X^θ)).

## 5. Structural evidence for YES

(T0) The both-prime-deleted graph is shattered at small scales (most vertices in micro-components;
(3,4), (4,5), (5,6) are isolated) but giant components grow with scale: ≈21.5% of valid vertices in
[2,3000]² lie in a single component pair. Combined with the unbounded verified chains of §2, the
answer is almost certainly YES; §4 localizes exactly what remains for a proof.
